The Egyptian fragrance market is not small, and it is not quiet. Oil suppliers, manufacturers,
independent brands, retail sellers, and customers all trade actively — across Facebook pages,
WhatsApp threads, Instagram DMs, and the occasional generic online store. The activity is real.
The infrastructure is not.

That gap is the whole project.

## What I actually observed

Before framing an opportunity, I mapped who was in the market and what each group needed before
they would join anything:

- **Suppliers and manufacturers** hold the raw materials, but have no structured way to be found
  by the merchants who need them. Their catalogue lives in a chat history.
- **Merchants and brand owners** want to sell finished perfume, but sourcing runs on personal
  relationships, and building a store means choosing a general tool that knows nothing about
  fragrance.
- **Customers** cannot easily compare, verify authenticity, or trust a seller whose entire
  storefront is a social page.
- **Everyone** repeats the same conversation — availability, price, minimum order, delivery —
  because none of it is structured data.

I had years of direct exposure to this category, which is the only reason the language,
workflows, and behaviours in the platform are right. That domain knowledge is not decoration on
this project; it is why the diagnosis holds.

## The diagnosis: fragmentation is structural, not cosmetic

It is tempting to read this market as "these businesses need websites." That reading produces a
store builder, and a store builder would fail here.

The problems were **trust**, **fragmented data**, **inconsistent presentation**, and
**marketplace liquidity**. Those are not website features. A merchant with a beautiful generic
store still cannot find a supplier. A supplier with a Shopify page still cannot be discovered by
the merchants who would buy from them. The gap sits *between* participants — which means no
single-participant tool can close it.

## Who participates, and why they join

A two-sided market fails if either side's value proposition is vague. So the pathways are not a
marketing section — they are the business model, made explicit and public.

Two things in that screen matter more than they look.

**First: B2B supply and B2C retail live in the same platform.** The supplier sells raw materials
to the merchant; the merchant sells finished perfume to the customer. Most platforms pick one.
Running both is harder — and it is the only way to close a gap that exists *between* those
groups.

**Second: no commission.** That single choice sets the entire operating model. Commission means
policing every transaction, and it punishes your best merchants for growing. Subscription means
the platform earns by being continuously useful, which is a harder promise and a cleaner
alignment. It also means the whole lifecycle — trial, activation, renewal, expiry, reactivation
— becomes core product surface rather than a billing detail.

## The supply-first problem

Every marketplace faces the same trap: buyers will not come before there is supply worth
browsing, and suppliers will not join before there is visibility worth having. Whichever side
you court first, the other one is your justification for existing — and it is empty.

So the sequence was decided before the build, and it is deliberately slow at the front:

1. **Supply quality first.** Onboard credible suppliers and sellers. Define the data standards.
   Make profiles worth discovering at all.
2. **Platform usefulness.** Improve inventory, search, category structure, store presentation,
   and trust signals until the experience justifies arriving.
3. **Demand growth.** Expand customer acquisition only once the platform can actually deliver a
   useful discovery and purchase experience.
4. **Retention and ecosystem.** Strengthen renewals, recurring value, content, reviews, and the
   relationships between participant groups.

The discipline here is resisting step 3. Spending on customer acquisition early would have
produced traffic, a good-looking chart, and a bad first impression that you only get to make
once.

product. Every additional field is a chance to lose someone who was willing.

## Governance is the unglamorous half

A marketplace is only as useful as its worst listing. The parts nobody photographs are the parts
that decide whether this works:

- **Structured business identities** — supplier, manufacturer, and store profiles with real data
  standards, because discovery is only as good as what it indexes.
- **Subscription and listing lifecycle** — activation, renewal, expiry, discontinued profiles,
  reactivation. With no commission, this *is* the revenue system.
- **Admin review and approval queues** — someone has to own quality before it reaches a customer.
- **Trust infrastructure** — review controls, content standards, moderation. In a market that
  ran on personal reputation, importing that trust is the product.
- **Search visibility** — built into the operating model, not appended as a marketing task
  after launch.

## Preventing feature bloat

Early platforms die from ambition more often than from competition. Every proposed feature was
put through the same four questions: what user value does it create, does it fit the strategy,
what does it cost to *operate* — not just build — and can the underlying problem be solved more
simply?

That last question killed more features than the other three combined. It is the same gate as
[Simplify → Automate → Build](/#sab): most requests are a symptom of something a rule, a
sequence, or a clearer sentence could resolve. Building it would have felt like progress and
added permanent maintenance.

## The business effect

Formula4You is a live, category-focused platform built around the real operating needs of
Egypt's fragrance ecosystem. The strongest evidence here is not a growth number — it is the
**completeness of the system**: a participant model with two coherent sides, an onboarding path
short enough to survive, a revenue model that aligns the platform with its merchants, a launch
sequence that refuses to buy demand it cannot yet serve, governance for the boring failure
modes, and a live product where all of it is visible.

I will not tell you how many suppliers are on it, how much has been sold through it, or how many
people visit. Not because those numbers are bad — because I have not published a source, a
baseline, a period, and a calculation for them, and without those a number is just a mood.

The platform is public. You can go and look.
